Point of View
    1. Explicitly engages the studio brief.
    2. Articulates a concrete, demonstrated user need (not a specific solution).
    Inspiration Quality
    1. At least 5 visual inspirations with clear explanations.
    2. Some inspirations show the best way the user could solve the problem today, and explain why it falls short.
    3. Other inspirations make a more abstract/metaphorical connection to the point of view.
    Format
    1. Prototypes & Storyboards are hand-drawn in clear black ink, without the use of computer tools.
    Storyboard #1
    1. Setting begins with a clear user need, and ends with a satisfaction addressing it.
    2. The sequence is easy to follow. Someone else could come up with a distinct prototype that would correspond with the point of view just from looking at the storyboard.
    Storyboard #2
    1. Setting begins with a clear user need, and ends with a satisfaction addressing it.
    2. The sequence is easy to follow. Someone else could come up with a distinct prototype that would correspond with the point of view just from looking at the storyboard.
    Prototype #1
    1. Presents an interface (at a sketch level) that accomplishes the design's major functionality.
    2. Paper prototype has enough detail that the user can see all of the interactions, understand how they work, and a programmer could use the prototype to create a functional application with a defined flow.
    3. Paper prototype connects to the point of view and Storyboard #1.
    Prototype #2
    1. Presents an interface (at a sketch level) that accomplishes the design's major functionality.
    2. Paper prototype has enough detail that the user can see all of the interactions, understand how they work, and a programmer could use the prototype to create a functional application with a defined flow.
    3. Paper prototype connects to the point of view and Storyboard #2.
    Outside the box (extra point)
    • Either one or both prototypes submitted were incredibly creative and innovative.
